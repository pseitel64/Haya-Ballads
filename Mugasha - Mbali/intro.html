The Place You Come From

Justinian Mugasha of Kibona

Begin Listening

Introduction

The Place You Come From is rich in poetic imagery. Metaphor, in fact, is the pivot on which the action of the ballad turns. As in the ballads King Kaitaba and Mugasha, the dramatic energy propelling the main plot is released by a controlled, explosive, verbal exchange in which an insult is conveyed to a person who will not stand for it. In The Place You Come From, the exchange is poetic and in the genre called oku-shambagiza, "to evaluate a person or social entity through metaphoric comparisons." In this genre, the evaluation is usually valorization, and the comparisons are praises. But in the case presented in the present ballad, the comparison is insulting. To prepare for that verbal detonation, to build its dramatic and semantic inevitability, the bard surrounds events leading up to it with a rich filigree of poetic imagery. After the insult, first to heighten and finally to resolve the explosive conflict it ignites, the poet reveals a socio-aesthetic ideology that disambiguates interpretation and rationalizes judgment, or nearly so. These poetic and narrative events are in the comic mode. All ends well for the protagonists who have our sympathy, and their way is marked with some amusement, at least for the audience. The ballad concludes with an extended metaphor on the same subject as that of the motivating insult, which is as life affirming and mirth producing as the insult is spiteful. The imagery and themes of the ballad come, of course, from Haya social imagination and poetic creativity, as these have been shaped for and by the ballad genre. The figures and topics themselves fit well in their performance settings. They are near at hand and on the mind at wedding celebrations, a king's court, or today's public bars. Justinian Mugasha performed this at a bar in Nyamugonga, in Kihanja, Kagera District, on December 1, 1968.
