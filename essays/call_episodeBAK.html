The Call Episode in Ballads: Narrative Logic and Principal Themes

As I noted before, the call episode can be described as an address to a protagonist that motivates principal action (every ballad in this collection has as least one). But this description only begins to comprehend the call's significance. Calls are not only events that trigger plot development. They are high points where dominant themes gather to frame ensuing events with significance and where poetic elaboration usually intensifies to mark this meaningful moment. Attending to these episodes and trying to understand how calls are narratively and stylistically constructed provide a way in for cultural outsiders to grasp a wealth of meanings that might otherwise remain obscure to them.

The call is key; observe carefully the social standing of the issuer and/or the recipient of the narrative calls to action, the rhetorical form used and the response (digital in-line annotations help when needed); you will begin to see the social contours and ethical conflicts that animate these heroic ballads and infuse them with meaning. In narrative calls a socially defined individual responds to institutional power and practice, and in that response we can read the complex ethics of a heroic age. The call articulates the cultural values that inform and situate subsequent actions, whether these come as inexorable linear pursuit or luxuriant digression. Bards often deploy elaborate poetic resources to heighten this crucial moment.

A Multiplicity of Calls

Calls come dressed in many rhetorical forms. Rarely are they as simple and direct as the war drums that summon the royal warrior Kachwenyanja from his newly married bliss to serve the state (this call is parodied in the last line of The Place You Come From). Calls may take the form of a seductive overture, as it does in two of the ballads, The Place You Come From and Kaiyula. This rhetorical act is inevitably and narratively complicated by relationships with persons beyond the potentially loving pair. A call may come in the form of an insult, as it does to the lowly fisher god Mugasha and to the King of Kyamutwala, Kaitaba, although we see that the latter call is a false report, fabricated by the king's own men. Or the call may be the true report of a devastating loss, as when Nyakandalo, the royal warrior's wife hears of his death in battle or when a young cowherd announces to all-powerful King Kitekere that his cattle herd has been raided. The call may come from a destitute stranger to wealthy clan patriarch Rukiza, requesting an audience and claiming blood brotherhood with him. The social identity of these initiators of calls is significant as is that of the child who comes to agricultural fields where women work to announce the apparent death of his father Kajango and to summon spirits to curse his mother for it.

Epic ballads do not usually present a single call followed by linear action. Instead, they construct their semantic richness dialogically through multiple calls deepening the conversation between opposing ethical claims. This multiplicity is not structural redundancy but dramatic necessity—the juxtaposition of different calls and responses enriches the epic's central conflicts.

Consider Rukiza and King Ruhinda. Rukiza receives a call from a poorly dressed stranger who appears in his cattle kraal claiming to have established blood brotherhood with him. Despite the man's suspicious appearance, Rukiza responds with generosity—having him properly clothed, granting him an audience, listening to his warning. In his response to this call, Rukiza displays the clan-related caring ethos that defines his heroic character. But Ruhinda also receives a call: his courtiers tell him (falsely) that Rukiza flaunts his cattle wealth in ways that insult royal authority. Ruhinda responds by ordering Rukiza's cattle confiscated. Two calls, two responses, two institutional ethics—and their collision drives the tragic action.

Similarly, both Kachwenyanja and his wife Nyakandalo receive calls. The warrior hears the royal war drums summoning him to battle—an institutional summons he cannot refuse without dishonoring his standing even though his newly married wife objects. His wife later receives her own call, responding to news of his death by plotting revenge. These paired calls create a dialogic structure where male and female, warrior and wife, occupy different institutional roles yet face parallel ethical decisions and loyalties. The epic's power derives not from a single heroic action but from the interplay of multiple calls. Similarly, the outrageously Cassanova-like would-be seducer of Kaiyula engenders a series of call-and-response exchanges after his initial confession of adoration and desire. This series of dialogues illuminates dimensions of clan related ethics, their supernatural protections, and an opinion on female sexuality.

Stylistic Elaboration at the Call

Calls are not only thematically dense but stylistically marked. They are the moments where bards often deploy their sophisticated poetic resources to signal that something crucial is happening.

Consider the call of the royal drums to Kachwenyanja. They come in an elaborate cascade of place-names that echo throughout the epic. Consider also his wife Nyakandalo's response to the call that sets her on the path of reenge. Her utterance is structured in a stanzaic complexity based on patterns of parallel phrases that carry both emotional weight and dramatic force. The formal elaboration marks the gravity of the moment: a heroic wife commits herself to vengeance against her husband's killers.

This elaborate patterning recurs across the tradition. When Tree Mwata refuses to be cut, it does not simply say "no" but enumerates, in a detailed catalogue, each type of vessel it rejects. The tree's eloquent defiance proclaims its power. When seducers call to young wives in Kaiyula and The Place You Come From, they employ dense metaphoric language, praise epithets, and sensual imagery. The poetic sophistication of the call mirrors its dangerous allure.

In folktales, compositional importance is often marked by a repeated song—the element audience members anticipate. In epic ballads, call and response usually signal "pay attention here" through poetic elaboration rather than repetition. The aesthetic effort proclaims the ethical weight. When bards pour their artistry into a call, they tell audiences: this moment matters, these words have consequences.

Reading a Call: The Cowherd and the King

The call in Kitekele demonstrates how attending to form and context unlocks understanding of meaning and appreciation of artistry that might otherwise escape notice. A cowherd comes before the powerful King Kitekele to announce that the royal herd has been raided. He tells the king, who had been relaxing with his favorites in a game of mankala, "That mankala game I would throw at you/ They are plundered [description of the herd]"

This seems straightforward—a bold, perhaps reckless statement from a subordinate to a ruler explicitly known for his violence, a hypothetical example of which was given a few lines before. But the phrase carries weight beyond its surface meaning. Earlier in the ballad, a spirit medium used this exact rhetorical form to chastise his own wife who had heedlessly wakened him from a prophetic dream. The parallelism is precise: If you had been [personal name] or [personal name] I'd have [done an aggressive act] (understood: as it is, I'll just yell at you)—not the way for a lowly cowherd to talk, especially to a violent king, even if you might get away with this kind of talk to a junior wife.

Yet the king does not respond with anger toward the cowherd or punishment. He mourns only the lost cattle, taking no offense at words that might constitute grave insult in another context. Why?

Because institutional ethics demand that in this moment his attachment to cattle exceed even his need to defend his own honor. His identity as guardian of the herds matters more than his dignity as an individual. The call depicts Kitekere's absolute love of and dedication to his cattle and portends how he will embody the institutional wrath that will set things straight.

Without attention to the rhetorical form of the call (matching the spirit medium's rebuke of his wife) and the king's specific response (grief for loss of cattle, not for honor), this bit of meaningful detail and artistic flourish remains hidden. Attending to the call points the way to deeper meaning and poetic achievement. This is only one aspect of the stylistic elaboration that occurs in the call episode of Kitekere.

This method of reading is valid across the tradition. False calls seem to cluster around kings, who -depend on informants and are thus susceptible to manipulation. Insulting calls test whether honor, rank, or duty will prevail. Multiple calls in a single ballad can show competing ethics in dialogue or collision. Always, the call crystallizes what matters a lot in the epic ballad: who is speaking, in what situation, to whom, and what is the response.

Conclusion

The call is where to look for important themes, ethical principles, and a heightened poetic emphasis that presages the consequences to follow. For cultural outsiders seeking valid readings, calls provide a valuable way in to the epic's thematic architecture. Attend the call, and the epic world opens.
