Why These Are Epics: The Heroic Age

I call these narratives epics because they are relatively long story-songs that depict action in a heroic society. I call them ballads because they are composed in recurring stanzaic patterns and sung to instrumental accompaniment. Together, "epic ballad" captures both their narrative scope and their poetic form.

The term "epic" has been contested in African literary studies. In the past, some scholars doubted whether oral epic poetry existed in sub-Saharan Africa at all. That debate has long been settled—African epics exist and flourish across the continent, from the Sunjata of the Mande to the Mwindo of the Nyanga to these Haya enanga performances. What matters in the present context is not judging whether these performances are formally epics, but understanding the nature of epic themes and the way they construct and construe conflict in an imagined society-- like Homer's Greece and others -- that can be called an heroic age.

The Heroic Age

Haya epic ballads construct a heroic age -- a literary resonse to a period in Haya history between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries during which Haya cultural identity was forged amid institutional conflict and accommodation between the centralized political power of kingship and the decentralized authority of patriarchal clans. An ascendant state ideology of loyal subordination and royal reward fought against the patrilineal clan ideology of kinship obligation and reciprocal exchange. The epics depict this tension in choices faced by individual protagonists who act in accordance with, or in opposition to, institutionally related ethical princoples

What defines a heroic age? The philosopher Alasdair MacIntyre, building on the work of classical scholar M.I. Finley, offers a useful framework. In heroic society, every individual has a given role and status within a well-defined system of roles and statuses. The key structures are kinship and household, but also—crucially for understanding these epics—emerging state power. A person knows who they are by knowing their role in these structures. And in knowing this, they know what they owe and what is owed to them.

This creates a distributive system of ethics—one in which individual rights and obligations vary according to social status and role. The obligations of an epic warrior differ from those of a king, a clan patriarch, or a common person. Each social standing requires its own corresponding ethical stance. When these obligations conflict, as they inevitably do when clan and state compete for land and loyalty, individuals face genuine moral dilemmas that cannot be resolved by appeal to a single universal code.

Epics are dramatic rather than didactic. They do not preach a simple message about right conduct. Instead, they explore the tragic or triumphant consequences of choices made in a world where institutional powers make competing claims. Rukiza must choose between loyalty to his new king and protection of his clan's property. Kachwenyanja's wife guilefully mixes clan and royal strategies to enact her revenge, overleaping the bounds of both structures. The king in "The Place You Come From" explicates metaphors and muses on the superior understanding of woman's role in a royal institutional setting. These kinds of ethical contradictoions are not simple and the epics honor their complexity.

Epic Characteristics

Epic differs from other narrative genres in its relationship to history. Folktales, in Haya tradition, operate in a timeless realm where ethical principles are universal and unchanging. A folktale hero succeeds through cleverness, persistence, and adherence to a single moral code. Proverbs, similarly, claim to distill universal wisdom applicable across situations.

But epics necessarily depict conflicts that grow from a particular historical period. The audience understands that these stories reflect the world as it was when clans and kingdoms contended for land and power, when warriors defended cattle-wealth, when royal courts punished miscreants and rewarded warriors and bards. This historical anchor gives epic its verisimilitude and its power to comment on enduring questions about authority, loyalty, and justice.

The performance context of epic accomodates this distinctive quality. In comparison with the warm exchanges around a cooking hearth where folktales might be told, epic performance is often cool and contemplative. A professional singer entertains a mostly adult audience. The invited listeners sit, sip millet beer, and listen, and the aesthetic distance thus created allows for reflection on the complexity of the values dramatized.

The Call Episode

A distinctive feature of Haya epic ballads is the centrality of what I call "the call"—an address to a protagonist that motivates the principal narrative action. In responding to it, the hero or heroine acts in a manner that embodies or challenges institutional ethics. The call motivates subsequent encounters and frames them with thematic significance.

Calls come in many forms: the royal war drums' roar heard by a loyal warrior, a neighbor's seductive proposition addressed to a young wife, an insulting challenge hurled by an enemy. The call might even articulate a lie. But whatever its form or content, the call requires a response, and that response reveals heroic character and precipitates action. The call is not merely a plot device—it is the moment when individual agency meets institutional power, when personal desire confronts social obligation.

This pattern—call and response leading to conflict and resolution—structures the dramatic architecture of all these epics ballads. The call is where dominant themes concentrate, where the ethical tensions of the heroic age manifest most clearly. Understanding the call is key to understanding the epics as both artistic achievement and historical witness.

Why Epics Matter

These epic ballads preserve in performance a collective memory of a formative period in Haya history. They are not straightforward records of past events but artistic representations of historical experience. Through them, we begin to understand the complex ways listeners think about the origins of their social world. In them, we contemlate the past's ethical contradictions and its trials of honor and loyalty seen explicitly and comfortably from afar. And as well we see the values, joys and fears shared across time.

The social conditions that created the heroic age depicted in the ballads is gone. Haya kingdoms were abolished at Independence, and clan power has diminished. Yet the epics continue to speak, for the questions they raise about authority, justice, honor, and the competing claims of different institutional ethics remain perennially human. The ballads transcend their historical moment yet remain deeply rooted in it—as all great epics do.
