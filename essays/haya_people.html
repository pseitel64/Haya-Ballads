The Haya People

The Haya people live in northwestern Tanzania, in the Kagera region along the western shore of Lake Victoria. This verdant landscape of rolling hills, banana groves, and rich agricultural land has been home to the Haya for centuries, supporting in the past a complex society organized around two powerful but sometimes competing institutions: patrilineal clans and centralized kingdoms.In the present, Haya are part of a modern state of Tanzania and engage in local, regional and world economies.

For many centuries, patrilineal clans were the dominant social institution in Hayaland. Through them, social groups regulated marriage and reproduction, inheritance, land ownership, dispute settlement, relations with the spirit world, and other important aspects of life. Each clan had its own totems, its own history, and its own relationship to the land. Clans provided identity, security, and structure to Haya life.

Alongside clan power, centralized kingdoms emerged between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries. During this period, Haya cultural life evolved under the influence of institutional opposition and accommodation between the decentralized authority of clans and the centralized political power of kingship. This tension between clan and state permeates many of the epic ballads, which are set in a heroic age of ethical conflict between these forces that shaped public life.

The relationship between clans and kingdoms was complex. Solidarity between them was promoted through hypergamous marriages, in which men from the king's clan would marry women from other clans, thus creating permanent, personal relationships between the groups involved. Clans were also integrated into the state as corporate entities through distinctive occupational specialties each performed at the royal court: one clan provided the guards, one provided the cooks, one provided the king's bathers, one provided the sorcerers who protected the king, and so on.

In the realm of religion, conflict and coexistence between clan and state was similar. The pre-state, clan-based religion of the Bachwezi spirits—a pantheon of gods who possessed ritual specialists and gave them powers to treat health and social problems—was augmented by spirits of deceased kings. And the royal state adopted for its relious practice a regionally popular nature deity Mugasha, god of Lake Victoria.Today, this religious belief and practice is part of a wider landscape of Muslim, Christian and other religions

By the time the epic ballads in this collection were recorded in the late 1960s, much had already changed. The kingship had been officially abolished in 1961 at Tanzanian independence. Clan relationships were still important, but their power was diminished by the modernizing forces of government, commerce, and religion, and by the educational system. Yet the ballads preserve in performance the memory of an earlier time—a heroic age when clans and kingdoms contended for land and power, when warriors defended cattle and honor, and when the enanga bards sang at royal courts and clan wedding celebrations.

Understanding this historical and social context enriches appreciation of the ballads. The conflicts depicted in these epics are not only the personal visions and aesthetic achievements of individual bards but also rhetorical statements about the broader historical conditions of Haya society. Heroes and heroines navigate a world where clan property and identity, royal reward and authority, personal standing and honor, and spiritual sanction and alliance can make competing claims on personal loyalty.
