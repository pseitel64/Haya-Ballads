Recording These Performances

Justinian Mugasha, the enanga bard, sat peacefully on a woven raffia mat spread for him on the rush floor in a village bar. He had done this many times before in his Kagera region of northwestern Tanzania. It was December 1, 1968, at 4:30 in the afternoon. The audience -- perhaps twenty men, women, and some children, mostly dressed in their Sunday best -- sat on chairs and benches around him. One man was actually not in his Sunday best—he had stopped in on his way home from fishing and was carrying a nice large freshwater fish to his family. Some members of the audience seemed slightly troubled by his presence.

At first, everyone sat quietly and listened to a battery-powered shortwave radio, which announced the news in Swahili from Radio Dar es Salaam. It was on for fifteen minutes. They also watched me set up my tape recording equipment.

I had been brought to this bar by Feregensi, a teacher of religion at Lyamahoro Lower Primary School and my neighbor. I had rented a house on that school compound for my field research with the help of Dr. Israel Katoke, the Haya historian, whom I met when he was finishing his PhD at Boston University and I was a graduate student at the University of Pennsylvania in folklore.

Proverbs were the initial topic of my research, which focused on the conversational use of metaphor. And by the time Feregensi brought me news of the musical event we were to attend, I had already begun to collect data, knowing I would find a rich lode of proverbs in the area. But I was happy to record any and all cultural performances available.

The news broadcast finished. My Nagra III tape recorder was ready. Justinian Mugasha began to play and sing. His instrument was the enanga—a shallow wooden trough zither strung with nylon strings that he plucked while singing narrative poetry.

Mugasha chose his songs that day, beginning with one about a recent king of Ihangiro kingdom, then moving to a much shorter comic ballad that had an enlivening effect on the audience. You can hear them begin to comment and exclaim on the tape as Mugasha builds the narrative. That second song—"Mbali Oluga Niyo Nzalwa" (The Place You Come from I Was Born)—became an important part of this collection.

That event, and the questions, especially about metaphor, that it generated, set me on a path leading initially to transcribing and explicating texts and locating other singers, and ultimately to describing, interpreting, and translating many works from the epic tradition. News of Mugasha's tape recorded performance spread locally. On several Sundays over the next year, visitors would come to my house and ask to hear it. Almost always I would accede (except when my supply of batteries was low), and the recording never failed to amuse and instruct the listeners.

But this session led to other sessions and questions. What was the tradition from which this came? Were there other singers, other types of songs? Was this a representative example? Was the singer among the best?

This last point arose almost immediately when I played the tape for Godfrey Ngaiza, my principal proverb teacher, a man highly regarded for his wisdom and his talent for using the Haya language. He said the singer was bad in voice, instrumental ability, and poetic talent. I was fortunate to have encountered a knowledgeable and honest critic so early in my research. His judgment of Mugasha, which I would eventually adjust, was coupled with advice on locating other enanga bards (To briefly set this matter straight, Abdullah Feza, singer-scholar of the tradition. listened to the tape in 1980 and declared that Mugasha's singing voice and playing might be lacking, but his words are first-rate).

The knowledge and opinion of native Haya speakers have shaped every aspect of this project. My method was this: I would record a performance, then a Haya speaker would transcribe it; another teacher and I wold review the transcription, and then that teacher or another and I would study the text and construct a rough Kiswahili translation, attending closely to the meaning of difficult passages. These teachers were principally Elias Kashula and Winifred Kisiraga. We probed the cultural context of the ballads that created their meaning — exploring references, customs, and values that informed the narrative. This collaboration and dialogue was essential, for the ballads are densely packed with cultural knowledge that an outsider could easily miss or misunderstand.

I always recorded singers performing before an audience of Haya speakers. The session never consisted of just the singer, the tape recorder, and me. This was to assure that the performer structured the performance for someone he knew understood the language and cared about the subject matter.

Over the course of my fieldwork, I recorded performances by several different bards in various contexts—in bars, in private homes, at informal gatherings. Each performance was unique, shaped by the particular singer's style, the audience's composition, and the occasion. Yet all drew from the same deep well of theme, style and repertoire that had been passed down through generations of enanga bards.

The process of moving from these tape recordings to the published texts and translations you encounter in this digital collection has been long and detailed. Each ballad required multiple sessions with Haya speakers, careful attention to linguistic nuance, research into aspects of cultural and historical context, and sustained effort to find English equivalents that could convey both the literal meaning and the poetic force of the Haya originals.

What emerges from this process I hope is not merely a collection of ancient stories but a window into a living tradition—one that, even as it adapted to changing social circumstances, continued to offer its audiences authentic reflections on the nature of power, love, and loyalty.
