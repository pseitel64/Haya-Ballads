Four Bards, Four Voices

The enanga tradition nurtured a grand variety of styles. Each individual bard -- supported by traditionally shaared poetic techniques, narrative structures, characteristic themes, and performance modes -- deployed his distinctive set of talents and capabilities. His individual creative faculty engaged all aspects of the bardic art—plot, theme, and linguistic stle, as well as vocal, instrumental, and dramatic performance.

The four bards represented in this collection—Habib Suliman, Muzee of Kanazi, Abdallah Feza, and Justinian Mugasha—have diffrent and worthy approaches to enanga art. Hearing their distinctive voices hones our appreciation both of specific ballads and of the contours of the tradition as a whole.

Habib Suliman: Innovator and Dramatist

Dr. Mugyabuso Mulokozi, the scholar who has studied Habib most extensively, identifies three characteristics of his artistry. He innovates rather than replicates within the genre. His particular talent lies in dramaturgy—evoking the presence of narrated events with a full repertoire of mimetic faculties, including vocal imitations and facial gestures. And he is, among the bards represented here, the best instrumentalist.

His innovative powers are clearly at play in his versions of Mugasha, especially in the depiction of the wedding party sent by the fisher-god to the high-god's residence. Innovative also is his version of the ballad Kajango, in which the protagonist who suffers the loss of a companion is male, in opposition to other versions I know, in which that protagonist is female.

Habib's performance is more openly theatrical than others. His talent is directed toward mimetic rather than purely poetic effects, although the latter is not lacking. Habib's Mugasha I features only about 60% of lines that cohere in stanaic patterns. But it also contains a poetic structure composed of the most lines by far. Especially in his 30-years-later version of the same ballad, Mugasha II (1980), Habib shapes his performance with more quoted speech genres, imitated voices, and interjections of his own theatrical presence.

Muzee of Kanazi: Poet of Dangerous Liaisons

Muzee's talent is also innovative. He composed and sang a humorous balladic commentary on America's Apollo 11 moon landing barely two weeks after the event, presenting a metaphoric analogy with a long folktale not found in any Haya collections I know. His self-presentation as a bard is captured in the lines of his poetic signature: "You are a danger./ You are a danger, Muzee./ And to play the enanga/ Perhaps is not [merely] a game" (Oli hatari./ Oli hatari, Muzee./ N'okutera enanga/ Shana si mchezo—a macaronic verse in Haya and Swahili, unlike any of his other verses, which are solely in Haya).

By playfully calling himself a danger, Muzee warns/boasts that the dramatic events he depicts are fraught with deeper social contradictions—an underlying danger in an apparent play of mere words. His portrayals are often animated by fiercely opposing passions: mother-love versus a woman's circumscribed but precious autonomy (Kajango), the reproductive urgings of a young bride's body versus her duties to her husband's clan (Kaiyula), a warrior's love for military virtue versus his love for a very special woman (Kachwenyanja). Muzee's art illuminates these contradictions without flly resolving them, making visible the ethical tensions that structure social life in a heroic age and perhaps today as well.

Abdallah Feza: the Pursuit of Excellence

Abdallah Feza seems to pursue his understanding of formal enanga perfection. In his version of Kaitaba, about 95% of the lines are stylistically elaborated with intricate patterns—the highest proportion among the bards represented here. This ballad depicts the power of words (and beer) to inflame passions and move monarchs to rash acts, and Feza's performance enacts that power through relentless poetic artistry.

Yet Feza is not limited to this approach. In another performance (Kitekere), his proportion of poetically patterned lines declines to 60%, similar to Habib's dramatic style. Kitekere centers on a dramatic tableau of warriors attending a heroic, physically dominant king at his royal court, whose easy camaraderie is suddenly shattered by the report of an enemy cattle raid. In this performance, dramaturgy becomes a larger part of the bard's style, and the audience is moved to contribute recitations of praise poetry appropriate to such a warrior gathering.

Like Habib, Feza shifts his artistic approach according to the narrative's demands—intense poetic cohesion for Kaitaba's verbal dueling, intense drama through quoted speech genres for Kitekere's battlefield warrior ethos. The tradition accomodates both modes of poetic creativity

Justinian Mugasha: Lean and Muscular

Justinian Mugasha's creative intellect, it seems to me, is almost completely focused on sharpening the power of his words. His ballads are lean and direct narratively and stylistically compared with the luxuriant elaboration in the works of other bards. He proceeds directly to the heart of the theme, with verse and narrative thrust fitting together tightly. In both his songs included here, the logic of action and the versification, sub-plots and poetic rhetoric march together to a seemingly inevitable resolution.

His choice of themes and his authorial perspective seem shaped by the same values as those represented by the fisher-god whose name the bard shares. There is an economy to Mugasha's art—no extraneous motion, no ornament for ornament's sake, just the essential elements arranged with precision. Where other bards elaborate, Mugasha sharpens his creative focus for clarity of aesthetic effect.

Diversity in the Tradition

These four voices suggest the breadth of artistic possibility within the enanga tradition. Habib's theatrical innovation, Muzee's passionate embrace of contradiction, Feza's poetic perfectionism, and Mugasha's robust economy—each talent is accommodated and nourished by a living enanga tradition. In these recorded performances one thus encounters not only traditional generic forms but also the work of individual artists who make coherent choices about how best to deploy their cultural inheritance. In each performance a bard brings his own gifts to a shared tradition..
