The Enanga Tradition

The enanga is a small wooden harp, technically called a trough zither, typically about two feet long, ten inches wide, and three inches deep. It is strung with a single, continuous nylon length of twine passed around notches in the trough ends to form eight or so harp-like strings. In the past the twine was made from cattle sinew. The musician holds the instrument in his lap, pressed against the outside bottom of a large empty calabash gourd for resonance, and plucks the strings with both hands, singing as he plays. The plucked strings create a low bass and medium treble melody that carries the narrative forward, commenting on and punctuating the sung text.

The word enanga refers both to the instrument itself and to the songs performed on it. This tradition of epic ballad singing has deep roots in Haya culture, though its social context has shifted over the past century.

The definitive scholarly work on the enanga tradition is Dr Mugyabuso M. Mulokozi's The African Epic Controversy: Historical, Philosophical, and Aesthetic Perspectives on Epic Poetry and Performance (Dar es Salaam: Mkuki na Nyota Publishers, 2002). Dr Mulokozi, himself a Haya and a scholar, conducted extensive fieldwork for his book, recording and analyzing performances by multiple bards and consulting with traditional authorities throughout the Kagera region. His comprehensive study analyzes the tradition's contextual, content, and formal features and provides a detailed examination of major epics, presenting full bilingual texts (Haya and English) alongside profiles of the singers and analyses of each epic's socio-historical context, literary content, and stylistic features. His work established the enanga tradition as a significant example of African epic poetry and remains the most thorough treatment of its performance contexts, poetic characteristics, and cultural significance.

Performance Contexts: Courts and Weddings

In the past, enanga performance was an occasional but important feature of weddings produced by clan leaders and a regular feature of entertainment at royal courts. At these points in the social field, enanga performance filled recurrent aesthetic and rhetorical needs. Here enanga bards perpetuated its legacy and added to it, and here patronage provided incentive for the bards to perfect their art.

These featured performances at weddings and courts seem to have been predominantly epic ballads—long narrative poems depicting heroic action, usually set in the past. At weddings, the epics most likely celebrated clan identity and values. At royal courts, they entertained the king and his retinue, sometimes subtly commenting on power and its proper exercise.

Performances at the royal court were held in the throne room, a straw-carpeted, circular space in a large, dome-shaped, thatch-built house, one of several functional buildings and residences that made up the fenced royal compound. At a king's request, and if a bard was available, performances began in late afternoon and lasted to late evening—until around midnight, with breaks between songs. One singer might provide a whole night's entertainment, or he might share the central focus with others. Competitions might be staged. A well-known bard might assemble a program of his pupils to introduce them to the court.

Favored performances met enthusiastic audiences, who murmured affirmation, reacted with laughter, snapped fingers in rhythm, sang along with commonly known songs quoted in a ballad, or might even rise to perform panegyric poetry when the heated context for this genre was depicted. Abdallah Feza told me that singers could create an elevated emotional state in which kings might sanction cattle raiding in a neighboring kingdom. Performances that did not achieve favor could be met with silence, inattention, or even—if the singer's choice of narrative proved offensive to a king—an interruption of proceedings and a threat of punishment (Mulokozi reports such an occurrence).

A king's reward for good performance might be a sojourn at the palace, some cattle, a parcel of royally-controlled land (nyarubanja), or—in Abdallah Feza's case—the gift of a wife (which he refused at first but then accepted, when advised by others including the two wives he had already married).

The Social Position of Bards

The enanga bards in this collection represent the last generation to have learned their art in the traditional contexts. Most enanga performers I met were farmers also, men who had learned the enanga as part of their cultural heritage and performed when opportunity and inclination coincided. Some performed more regularly than others; all four bards gathered here commanded enthusiastic audiences.

But two of the four singers represented in this collection were not landowners. This may have been a modern trend brought about by monetized exchange and the emergence of public bars as economic institutions capable of supporting occupational specialties. Or landless men may have been drawn to singing, and landlessness may always have been part of the tradition.

Bards were bound to the king in a different way from that of other craft specialists. The latter embodied clan obligations to the royal state, in which clans assumed a variety of duties in the royal household. This panoply of clan-identified workers included specialties like ironworkers, cooks, guards, rainmakers, sorcerers, barkcloth makers, hunters, and court jesters. But enanga bards are not listed among them in traditional accounts, although well-known bards were often resident at the royal court and performed regularly there.

Bardic performance was not part of any clan's corporate responsibilities to the state because its knowledge and talent did not necessarily flow patrilineally from father to son, as other arts and skills did, and as musical performance did in some societies in West Africa. In Hayaland, epic art was not associated with family, clan or caste. Individual predisposition and intense interest reproduced this art from generation to generation. This suggests that epic and kingship have not been married as long in Hayaland as they have in West Africa. It also suggests that the epic bard's poetic talent gave him greater independence from clan and king than that enjoyed by other artisans. But the economics of land and patronage kept him in the embrace of both institutions.

Regional Variations

The tradition varied from kingdom to kingdom. Ballad content—plots and themes—differed regionally because of the social milieu in which bards composed and performed. Each kingdom had its own royal lineage, and individual kings occasioned compositions about their forebears and themselves. The provenance of these royal ballads, however, was not completely limited by the boundaries of a kingdom. The bard Muzee, whose repertoire seems to represent the kingdom of Ihangiro, performed the ballad of Kaitaba, a former king of Kyamutwala. His version was musically and poetically apt, but not as accomplished and complete as that of Abdallah Feza, a resident of Kyamutwala and a bard well known at its court.

Thematic emphasis in epic ballads varied from south to north because of variations in the relative powers of state and clan. In general, the royal state was stronger and more stable in the northern kingdoms, less stable and less dominant over clans in the south. Bards from the southern kingdom of Ihangiro (Justinian Mugasha's tradition is based there and Muzee's seems heavily influenced by it) give greater prominence to clan-related themes. This is clearly seen in different versions of the fisher-god Mugasha's epic, in which spirits that embody high (state) and low (clan) social standing are in conflict. As Mulokozi has also observed, in southern versions Mugasha's treatment of his social betters is more outrageous and anti-royal in its connotations. The southern tradition also seems richer in themes of sexuality and reproduction, a domain governed largely by the ethical power of clans and perhaps appropriate themes for wedding celebrations.

These variations in the content of ballad tradition indicate differences in the points in the Haya social landscape where ballads were composed, performed, and recomposed. Simply put, in the northern kingdoms, generous patronage was to be found more in performances at a royal court relatively stable over generations. In the south, patronage rested to a greater degree in performances in private residences and at large weddings, which embody and enact clan ethical power.

Performance in the Present: Transformation and Continuity

By 1968, when I made the earliest of my recordings, the tradition had undergone significant transformation. The kingship had been abolished in 1961, and enanga performance at clan-sponsored wedding celebrations had seemingly disappeared. The enanga tradition had shifted to new venues: public bars for commercial clientele and private homes for family and neighbors.

This shift added new items to the bards' repertoire, principally narration of and commentary on noteworthy contemporary happenings. A bard might sing about a scandalous king (as Justinian Mugasha did in his song about a king who brought a prostitute to his palace in his Mercedes-Benz), Idi Amin's war, or the American moon landing, or about other events that captured public imagination. Yet the old epic ballads remained in the repertoire, and skilled bards continued to perform them alongside the newer topical songs.

All carried forward a tradition that stretched back centuries, adapting it to new social circumstances while preserving its essential features. In comparison with the warm interaction around a cooking hearth where folktales might be told, epic performance seems cool and contemplative. A professional singer entertains a mostly adult audience and unless the context is very informal, there is usually no conversational exchange or spontaneous interruption. The audience listens as the epic bard creates an aesthetic space for reflecting on the human condition, on the values and conflicts that have always animated Haya society.
